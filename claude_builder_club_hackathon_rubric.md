# Claude Builder Club Sprint Hackathon Judging Rubric

**Theme:** Social Impact inspired by Dario Amodei’s essay *Machines of Loving Grace*  
**Core idea:** Build AI projects that genuinely contribute to human flourishing while taking ethical tradeoffs seriously.

---

## 1. Hackathon Context

Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei’s essay *Machines of Loving Grace* describes a hopeful future where AI helps humans flourish by eliminating disease, expanding opportunity, strengthening democracy, and helping people find meaning.

This hackathon asks Claude Builder Club members to prototype projects that move toward that kind of future. Projects should show how AI can help people while wrestling with the hard ethical questions that come with powerful technology.

Judges should evaluate not only the code, but also the team’s thinking: who they are building for, why the problem matters, how the AI helps, what could go wrong, and how the team keeps humans in control.

---

## 2. Hackathon Tracks

Teams should place their project in one of the following five focus areas:

### A. Biology & Physical Health
Projects that make healthcare more accessible or help people manage physical health.

Examples of relevant goals:
- Improving access to health information or care navigation
- Helping patients understand or manage symptoms, medication, appointments, or recovery
- Supporting clinicians, caregivers, or public health workers without replacing professional judgment

### B. Neuroscience & Mental Health
Projects that expand access to mental health support, emotional understanding, or cognitive well-being.

Examples of relevant goals:
- Helping people reflect on mood, stress, or habits
- Supporting access to mental health resources
- Helping caregivers, counselors, students, or researchers better understand mental health patterns
- Providing non-clinical support while clearly avoiding diagnosis or unsafe overreach

### C. Economic Empowerment & Education
Projects that remove barriers to learning, work, financial mobility, or opportunity.

Examples of relevant goals:
- Personalized learning tools
- Career navigation or skills development
- Support for underserved learners, job seekers, workers, or entrepreneurs
- Tools that increase agency rather than automate people out of opportunity

### D. Governance & Collaboration
Projects that help people participate in democracy, deliberate better, coordinate action, or work together more effectively.

Examples of relevant goals:
- Making civic processes more understandable
- Helping communities organize, deliberate, or make decisions
- Improving transparency, accountability, or public participation
- Supporting collaboration without manipulating or overriding people’s choices

### E. Creative Flourishing
Projects that amplify human creativity and help people find meaning.

Examples of relevant goals:
- Creative tools that help people express themselves
- Systems for storytelling, art, music, writing, or reflection
- Tools that preserve human authorship and agency
- Projects that expand access to creative participation

---

## 3. Universal Principles for All Tracks

Judges should look for projects that demonstrate the following principles:

1. **Real problems for specific people**  
   The project should address a concrete problem faced by a clearly identified population, not a vague or hypothetical problem for “everyone.”

2. **AI that empowers rather than replaces**  
   The AI should help users understand, decide, create, learn, coordinate, or act. It should not remove human agency or make consequential decisions without meaningful user control.

3. **Serious ethical reasoning**  
   The team should understand who benefits, who might be harmed, what could go wrong, and what safeguards are needed.

4. **Working prototype**  
   The project should demonstrate core functionality. It does not need to be production-ready, but the demo should show the central idea working.

5. **Clear understanding of the problem and approach**  
   The team should be able to explain what they built, why they built it, how it works, and what they would do next.

---

## 4. Critical Questions Every Team Must Answer

Judges should expect every team to answer these questions clearly:

1. **Who are you building this for, and why do they need it?**
2. **What could go wrong, and what would you do about it?**
3. **How does this help people rather than make decisions for them?**

Optional follow-up questions:

- What specific user did you have in mind while building?
- What evidence do you have that this is a real problem?
- Why is AI necessary or useful here?
- What should the AI not be allowed to do?
- Where does human judgment remain essential?
- What assumptions are you making about users, institutions, or incentives?
- What would need to be true for this to scale responsibly?

## 7. Category 1 — Impact Potential

**Question:** Is this a real problem? Who does it affect and why does it matter? Does the solution make sense?

**Maximum score:** 25 points

| Score Range | What It Looks Like |
|---:|---|
| **21–25** | Addresses a specific, meaningful problem with a clear population in mind. You immediately understand who benefits and why this matters. The solution directly addresses the problem in a way that makes sense. |
| **13–20** | Addresses a real problem, but the population or benefit is somewhat vague. The connection between solution and problem is logical but could be stronger. |
| **6–12** | Problem is loosely defined or the solution does not clearly address it. Hard to tell who this is really for or why they would use it. |
| **0–5** | Problem is unclear or the solution has little connection to it. |

**Score:** _______ / 25

### Impact Evaluation Prompts

Use these prompts to guide scoring:

- Is the target user or community specific?
- Does the team explain why this problem matters?
- Is the need urgent, painful, frequent, or underserved?
- Does the project create a plausible benefit for the target users?
- Would real people actually want or use this?
- Could the idea scale beyond the hackathon while preserving its value?

## 8. Category 2 — Technical Execution

**Question:** Does the core functionality work? Does it demonstrate the idea effectively?

**Maximum score:** 30 points

| Score Range | What It Looks Like |
|---:|---|
| **25–30** | Core functionality works and effectively demonstrates the idea. Uses AI in a way that feels purposeful and well-considered. You can see how it would actually work for a real user. |
| **16–24** | Core functionality mostly works. AI is used adequately. Some gaps in the demo, but the concept comes through clearly. |
| **6–15** | Significant gaps in functionality or the demo does not clearly show how it works. AI use feels superficial or tacked on. |
| **0–5** | Does not work or is a mockup only. Little evidence of meaningful technical implementation. |

**Score:** _______ / 30

### Technical Evaluation Prompts

Use these prompts to guide scoring:

- Does the prototype demonstrate the core user flow?
- Does the AI component actually improve the product?
- Is the AI use purposeful rather than added for novelty?
- Does the system handle realistic inputs?
- Are outputs understandable and useful?
- Are there guardrails, feedback loops, or uncertainty handling where appropriate?
- Is the implementation coherent enough that the project could be extended after the hackathon?

### Strong AI Use Looks Like

Strong AI use may include:

- Summarizing complex information in a user-centered way
- Helping users reflect, decide, learn, create, or coordinate
- Making information more accessible across language, literacy, expertise, or disability barriers
- Personalizing support while respecting user control
- Surfacing options, tradeoffs, or explanations rather than making opaque decisions

Weak AI use may include:

- A generic chatbot with no clear purpose
- AI outputs that are not grounded in the user’s problem
- Automation that removes user agency
- Overclaiming accuracy, authority, or safety
- No handling of uncertainty, errors, or risky outputs

## 9. Category 3 — Ethical Alignment

**Question:** Did the team think seriously about potential harms? Does this empower people rather than replace them?

**Maximum score:** 25 points

| Score Range | What It Looks Like |
|---:|---|
| **21–25** | Team clearly wrestled with ethical implications. Can speak to who might be harmed, what could go wrong, and what they have done about it. Solution puts people in control. Ethical reasoning feels genuine, not performative. |
| **13–20** | Team considered some ethical implications and has reasonable answers. Solution is broadly empowering. Some gaps in ethical reasoning but shows genuine awareness. |
| **6–12** | Ethical considerations feel like an afterthought. Team struggles to articulate potential harms or safeguards. |
| **0–5** | No meaningful ethical consideration. Solution risks disempowering or harming users with no acknowledgment. |

**Score:** _______ / 25

### Ethical Evaluation Prompts

Use these prompts to guide scoring:

- Who could be harmed if this system fails?
- What happens if the AI gives a bad, biased, misleading, or overconfident output?
- Does the team clearly define what the system should not do?
- Does the product preserve human agency and user choice?
- Are users informed about limitations?
- Are there safeguards for high-stakes use cases?
- Does the project expand access equitably, or could it widen existing gaps?
- Does the team avoid replacing professionals, communities, or relationships where human judgment matters?

### Ethical Red Flags

Judges should be cautious if a project:

- Makes medical, legal, financial, or mental health decisions without professional oversight or clear disclaimers
- Claims to diagnose, treat, or predict sensitive outcomes without appropriate evidence
- Manipulates users emotionally or politically
- Automates decisions that should remain human-led
- Collects sensitive data without a clear reason or privacy plan
- Ignores bias, access, failure modes, or misuse
- Treats ethics as a slide at the end rather than part of the design

### Ethical Strengths

A strong project may:

- Keep the user in control
- Explain uncertainty and limitations
- Ask for user confirmation before consequential actions
- Provide options instead of single authoritative answers
- Include escalation paths to humans or professionals
- Minimize sensitive data collection
- Make benefits accessible to underserved users
- Show humility about what the system can and cannot do

## 13. Suggested Judging Process

1. **Listen for the problem and user.**  
   Before judging the tech, identify who the team is building for and why the problem matters.

2. **Watch the demo carefully.**  
   Look for working core functionality, not just a polished pitch.

3. **Evaluate whether AI is necessary and well-used.**  
   Strong projects use AI to empower users, not just to add novelty.

4. **Ask about failure modes.**  
   Every team should be able to explain what could go wrong and what they would do about it.

5. **Score each category independently.**  
   A project can be technically strong but ethically weak, or ethically thoughtful but technically incomplete.

6. **Reward honest scope.**  
   A focused prototype that solves a real problem for a specific group should score better than a broad idea that claims to help everyone.

---

## 14. Quick Reference: What Great Projects Usually Have

A high-scoring project usually has:

- A specific user or community
- A real, meaningful problem
- A working prototype
- AI that is central to the solution
- Clear human control and agency
- Thoughtful safeguards
- Honest discussion of limitations
- A clear path for future development
- A presentation that explains the problem, product, impact, and ethics clearly

---

## 15. Quick Reference: Common Reasons to Score Lower

A project may score lower if:

- The target user is vague
- The problem feels hypothetical
- The AI use is generic or superficial
- The demo does not work
- The project makes high-stakes claims without safeguards
- Ethical risks are ignored or minimized
- The team cannot explain how the system helps people retain control
- The presentation is hard to follow
- The solution seems disconnected from the stated problem

---

## 16. Final Judge Reminder

This hackathon is not only about building with AI. It is about showing how AI could help humans flourish.

The strongest teams should demonstrate:

- Technical creativity
- Real-world usefulness
- Respect for human dignity
- Clear ethical reasoning
- A working product that helps people do something they care about

Reward projects that are ambitious, but also grounded, specific, and honest about their limits.
