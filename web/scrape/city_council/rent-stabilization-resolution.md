The Cambridge City Council passed a resolution on February 9, 2026, formally calling on the Massachusetts Legislature to pass enabling legislation that would allow cities and towns to enact local rent stabilization ordinances. The resolution passed 7–2, with Councilors Toner and Simmons voting against.

## Background

Massachusetts is one of approximately 30 states that prohibits local rent control by state statute. Chapter 40P of the Massachusetts General Laws, passed in 1994 following a ballot initiative, bars municipalities from enacting rent control ordinances. As a result, Cambridge — which had rent control until 1994 — cannot independently reinstate it without the Legislature first repealing or amending Chapter 40P.

Rent stabilization enabling legislation has been filed in the Massachusetts Legislature in multiple sessions. The most recent bill, HD.1560/SD.1032, would allow municipalities to enact rent stabilization covering units in buildings with more than six units, with annual rent increases capped at the Consumer Price Index plus 3%.

## What the resolution states

The resolution:
- Formally expresses the Cambridge City Council's support for rent stabilization enabling legislation.
- Calls on Cambridge's state legislative delegation (currently Senators Connelly and Jehlen; Representatives Barber, Decker, Garballey, and Rogers) to actively support and vote for such legislation.
- Requests that the Mayor transmit a copy of the resolution to the clerks of both chambers of the Massachusetts Legislature.

## Arguments

Supporters argued that Cambridge's median rent has increased over 40% in inflation-adjusted terms over the past decade, that renters — who make up approximately 65% of Cambridge households — are disproportionately burdened by rising costs, and that rent stabilization would provide stability for long-term residents facing displacement.

Opponents (Toner and Simmons) argued that rent stabilization reduces housing supply by discouraging new construction, that the more effective approach is to build more housing, and that state preemption law reflects a considered policy judgment that should not be circumvented.

The resolution carries no local legal force but signals the council's position to state lawmakers. Housing is Cambridge's most frequent council agenda topic.
