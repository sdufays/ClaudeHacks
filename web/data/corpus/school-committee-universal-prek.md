The Cambridge School Committee voted 6–1 on March 16, 2026 to approve a plan expanding Universal Pre-K (UPK) eligibility to children who turn three years old by September 1, 2026. Cambridge currently provides UPK to all four-year-olds; this expansion represents a significant step toward universal care from age three.

## What changes

Under the approved plan:
- All Cambridge resident children who turn three by September 1 will be eligible for the UPK program beginning in the 2026–2027 school year.
- The three-year-old program will operate from 8:00 AM to 2:00 PM (half-day), with extended day options available through contracted community-based organization (CBO) partners.
- Enrollment priority is maintained for children with Individualized Education Programs (IEPs), children experiencing homelessness, children in foster care, and children from households below 185% of the federal poverty level.

## Cost and funding

The expansion adds approximately 320 three-year-old seats to the UPK system. Estimated annual cost: $4.8 million, to be funded through a combination of:
- The FY27 Cambridge Public Schools operating budget (pending full council adoption).
- Massachusetts Universal PreK grant funds (the city applied for $1.2 million in state grants in January 2026).

City Manager Huang's FY27 budget submission (April 29, 2026) also includes $5 million for early childcare for children ages 0–2 — the year group not yet reached by the UPK expansion.

## Context

Cambridge adopted UPK for four-year-olds in 2015. Waiting lists have consistently exceeded available seats for three-year-olds. High-quality early childhood education has significant documented benefits for child development outcomes, particularly for children from lower-income families. Cambridge's expansion is consistent with a broader state push toward universal early education following the Massachusetts Act Relative to Driving Clean Energy and Offshore Wind (2022), which included early education funding provisions.

The one dissenting vote (Councilor ex officio Siddiqui's proxy vote) raised concerns about whether the CBO partner capacity was sufficient to deliver high-quality extended-day programming at the planned scale.
