The Cambridge City Council voted 6–3 on January 27, 2026 to approve a zoning amendment allowing buildings up to six stories along the Cambridge Street corridor from Inman Square to Lechmere Square. The vote follows years of planning department study and public process, and represents the most significant upzoning along a major Cambridge commercial corridor in more than a decade.

## What changed

Under the old zoning, most parcels along Cambridge Street in this stretch were capped at three to four stories under the base Business B district rules. The amendment creates a new Cambridge Street Overlay District that:

- Allows as-of-right construction up to six stories (approximately 65 feet) on parcels with 50 feet or more of street frontage.
- Requires ground-floor commercial or community space on Cambridge Street-facing frontages.
- Sets an inclusionary zoning requirement of 20% affordable units for any residential project with more than 10 units, consistent with citywide policy.
- Establishes design standards emphasizing active street frontages, stepbacks above the fourth floor, and materials compatible with the existing neighborhood fabric.

## Vote breakdown

The six councilors voting in favor were: Siddiqui, Azeem, Zuber, Toner, Pickett, and Wilson. Voting against were: Nolan, Simmons, and McGovern, who each cited concerns about the pace of neighborhood change and the adequacy of community process, particularly in the predominantly residential blocks near Inman Square.

## Context and arguments

Supporters argued the corridor is well-served by transit — the 91 and 83 bus lines run along Cambridge Street, and the Green Line Extension (Lechmere and Union stations) provides rapid transit access — making it an appropriate location for increased residential density. They also cited Cambridge's housing shortage and the need to add supply near job centers.

Opponents raised concerns about displacement of existing businesses and long-term renters in the corridor's mixed residential-commercial buildings, arguing that upzoning without stronger anti-displacement measures could accelerate turnover. Several community members from the Inman Square Neighborhood Association testified in opposition.

This vote is a direct follow-on to the February 2025 citywide multifamily zoning reform, which changed zoning near transit stops across Cambridge to allow multifamily housing by right. The Cambridge Street amendment extends similar logic to the corridor as a whole, not just transit-proximate parcels.
