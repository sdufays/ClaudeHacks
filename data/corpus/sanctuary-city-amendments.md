The Cambridge City Council voted unanimously (9–0) on April 28, 2026 to adopt amendments to the city's existing sanctuary city ordinance. The amendments explicitly prohibit Cambridge city employees and police from assisting or cooperating with federal immigration enforcement agencies — including U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) and Customs and Border Protection (CBP) — while those agencies are conducting immigration enforcement activities on city-owned or city-controlled property.

## What the amendments do

The adopted amendments:

1. **Bar city employees** from providing information about the immigration status of any individual to federal immigration authorities unless required by a valid court order.
2. **Prohibit use of city resources** — including Cambridge Police Department personnel, facilities, vehicles, and databases — in support of federal civil immigration enforcement operations.
3. **Restrict access to city property** for federal immigration enforcement purposes without a judicial warrant signed by a federal magistrate or district court judge. An administrative ICE detainer alone does not constitute sufficient legal authorization.
4. **Require notification**: If a federal agency requests access to city facilities for immigration enforcement and is denied, the city's legal department must be notified within 24 hours.
5. **Establish a complaint process** for residents who believe city employees have violated these provisions.

## Background

Cambridge has maintained a sanctuary city policy since 1985. The 2026 amendments were introduced in response to increased federal immigration enforcement activity in the region following changes in federal administration policy. Councilors stated the amendments were designed to clarify and strengthen existing protections, not to create entirely new policy.

## Arguments made

Supporters argued the amendments protect immigrant residents — estimated at roughly 30% of Cambridge's population — from fear of deportation, and that effective local policing depends on community trust that cannot exist if residents fear their local police will collaborate with immigration authorities.

No councilor voted against the amendments, though some noted the city could face federal funding pressure as a result of the enhanced sanctuary provisions and expressed the need for ongoing legal review of the ordinance's interaction with federal law.
